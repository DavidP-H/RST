Power Supplies

The power supply is responsible for taking power from the outlet in the wall and turning it into something useable for the computer. It converts A/C power into D/C power as well as providing stable current to all parts of the computer, which is very important to prevent damage.

Power supplies have two main measurements, the wattage and the efficiency rating. A standard power supply is rated for around 500 watts.

Efficiency is measured using the 80+ standard. An 80+ power supply must be at least 80% efficient under 20%, 50%, and 100% load. After that, there are many other classifications 80+ bronze, silver, gold, platinum and titanium, which each improves on the efficiency by a few percent.

There are also different modularities, such as non-modular (where the cables are hard-wired into the power supply) semi-modular (where only the cables that are absolutely necessary (such as the motherboard 24pin and CPU connector) are hard-wired) and the rest (SATA power, PCI-e, etc.) are not hard-wired and can instead be unplugged if not being used, which helps with cable management.
